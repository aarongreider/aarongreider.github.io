The Jabberwocky

I created six illustrations bound together in a booklet based on the classic nonsense poem by Lewis Carroll.

It's Nonsense

’Twas brillig, and the slithy toves
Did gyre and gimble in the wabe:
All mimsy were the borogoves,
And the mome raths outgrabe.

“Beware the Jabberwock, my son!
The jaws that bite, the claws that catch!
Beware the Jubjub bird, and shun
The frumious Bandersnatch!”

He took his vorpal sword in hand;
Long time the manxome foe he sought—
So rested he by the Tumtum tree
And stood awhile in thought.

And, as in uffish thought he stood,
The Jabberwock, with eyes of flame,
Came whiffling through the tulgey wood,
And burbled as it came!

One, two! One, two! And through and through
The vorpal blade went snicker-snack!
He left it dead, and with its head
He went galumphing back.

“And hast thou slain the Jabberwock?
Come to my arms, my beamish boy!
O frabjous day! Callooh! Callay!”
He chortled in his joy.

’Twas brillig, and the slithy toves
Did gyre and gimble in the wabe:
All mimsy were the borogoves,
And the mome raths outgrabe.




Even before I began my analysis of the poem, I knew that I wanted to take a the poem on a walk down the opposite direction of Disney’s or Tim Burton’s take on the Alice in Wonderland canon. I wanted to make it decisively darker by drawing parallels between the actions of the hero and the sins of man. I adapted stylistic elements from the late horror artist Bernie Wrightson to create a more dubious and brooding atmosphere.

The poem is comprised of seven stanzas (I illustrated the latter six), which I drew parallel to each of the seven deadly sins in my planning process - in the order of Greed, Lust, Sloth, Envy, Wrath, Pride, and Gluttony respective to each stanza. These attributes shaped how I presented each illustration emotionally.

Analysis

The first Illustration deals with the hero’s greed manifest upon being sent to conquer and destroy.

The second represents his sloth at the base of the Tumtum tree. The scene presents his action of sloth and juxtaposes it with his persistence to act on greed. I placed emphasis on his hand to show it as his “vorpal sword,” the lack of a weapon is a metaphor for brutality of his own doing.

Acting on it’s envy, the Jabberwocky releases itself upon the hero where it is struck down in the name of the hero’s wrath.

The fourth illustration is from the Jabberwocky’s perspective to emphasize it as the victim of wrath as well as its own envy, and to evoke a sense of danger as the eyeless hero stares straight at the viewer.

Prideful as his conquest is completed, he arrives home to celebrate his success and fame.

In a deadly irony, his pride leads him to a dangerous intoxication.

I created a translation of the first and last stanza to clearly portray my interpretation:

It’s sunny, and the tree branches
Did sway in the wind:
All happy are our friends
And the wildlife reaches out

As the first stanza, it sets a scene of a forest with plentiful wildlife and coexisting establishment. As the last stanza, it shows nature running its course on the hero who, too intoxicated to defend himself, is graben by the force he grabed.

The booklet I created was intended to be a maze to flip through, making the viewing experience of the poem as confusing as the language used.
