AARON GREIDER

Body Mantle

The Poplar Prince’s robes were created from 101 twelve-inch poplar planks and held together with wood adhesive. The initial concept was modeled and rendered in Autodesk Maya, and the succeeding mantle was a loose adaption of what I could complete over the course of one all-nighter.

101 Poplar Planks and Wood Glue

The model was meant to liken cascading and asymmetrical movement a nontraditional ebb from bottom right to upper left culminating in a fractal spiral on the left shoulder.

Because the poplar planks resembled reeds, I researched styles of traditional reed armor - especially that of ancient eastern shoguns. I began by creating a duct tape bust of my torso that I could use to brainstorm, though, I opted to brainstorm in the digital space instead. When I began to construct the mantle, I found that the skeleton made a better bust than my poplar-reinforced duct tape one. To create dimension of the overall form, a tiny (>.25in) spacer made of cut, layered, and glued poplar was placed between each plank. Without the spacers there would be no way to glue the sticks together. Each joint was held together for 30 minutes with a clothespin to avoid damage before the adhesive sealed.

Poster Studies

Given a set body of text and a rigid corresponding hierarchy, I created four 11 x 17 graphic arrangements while adhering to the University of Cincinnati branding guidelines. Through this process, I gained the ability to justify my design decisions through rational and topical relevance while conveying the identity of an organization.

Iteration After Iteration

From the project guidelines, “Using the hierarchy presented below, develop a grid system that accommodates all of the following information in a concise and meaningful way. You must include the appropriate university logo. Use the font ‘Myriad Pro’ instead of the typeface specified in the Branding Guidelines.
Full project document here.

This project spanned the entire second-half of the Typography 1 semester; shown here in the consequent absurdly enormous amount of work we have to show for it. For the first many iterations, we were charged to design by a grid, and avoid particular graphic elements. Eventually, we were encouraged to spread out, to put personality and a wider variety of brand elements into our composition.

I pursued the idea of implied space and depth for my first graphic poster. For the appearance of a clean and finalized result, I used Autodesk Maya to emulate studio-lit photography with renderings that implied the depth of the paper. For a paper-like effect to project text on, I used 2- dimensional planes.

Included with the University branding guidelines, a folder full of themed monograms to be used over photography and colored backgrounds to provide visual interest and brand affiliation. I dipped into the Blue Ash College monogram, as well as circular elements to divide space and emphasize areas of the hierarchy.

I believe these designs were my least successful for two reasons: first, the monograms became the visual focus - fighting the content and failing to show university affiliation. Second, the visual elements failed to reflect the content and purpose of the poster, to sell the Communication Design program. I later realized while I was coming up with my final design, that this was a problem with my 2D plane iteration, too.

I needed to find something which reflected what I was designing for, something of an overarching metaphor.

I was sitting at Panera, being a basic Panera-goer eating my bread bowl and sipping my tea. In a uniquely design student fashion, I flagged a booth as my territory and immediately spread all my supplies everywhere (riding the risk of spilling my soup all over my binder). The mess, I realized, was composed of widely used tools for cultivating the creative process by many, I imagine, across the globe. If my Bic ballpoint pen, laptop and mouse, scrap paper and lunatic scrawl could be universally recognized as a setup for design, then I should be able to seize such symbolism as content for my poster.

Our professor had been encouraging us to utilize the grid as a part of our graphic composition, but some of his suggestions struck me as somewhat arbitrary. I thought about the halftone grid, an element associated with the pop art and grunge design movements, it seemed like the perfect solution for my design.

In the Style of Neville Brody

One of our final class projects involved an imaginary scenario: each of the students has been hired by PBS to design a poster for an episode in an upcoming series about the history of design. Each episode will cover a different designer who led the design of their time.

"The Message is the Medium"

Naturally, we drew designers out of a hat. I drew a particular rebel named Neville Brody.

Known for his early work for Fetish Records and his design for The Face Magazine in the 80’s and - later in the age of the internet - his leadership in digital font creation, dissemination, and usage thereof through FUSE and FontShop.

In his early days, Brody was a punk. He stayed on the forefront of design by experimentation with the abstract and precedent. He disdained his professors’ instruction, as they deemed his work “uncommercial.” He was almost expelled from The Royal College of Art in London after placing the Queen’s face sideways on a postage stamp design.

While designing the poster, I stayed true to Brody’s attention to the defining philosophy of each project that is consistent in all his work. Through each unique iteration, I explored several of his unique works created through different points in his career, eventually settling on the style of his early work of the 80’s.

While leafing through The Graphic Language of Neville Brody, a compilation and analysis of Brody's work by Jon Wozencroft, I came across a very intriguing set of album covers featuring a very gritty and inaccessible halftones and near-tribal markmaking - a motif and interest which spans brody's entire career. We were required to include an image of one of our designer's work, so I selected Stephen Mallinder's Pow Wow and integrated it's style with my work.

Each of the fonts used in the posters are Neville Brody's own, affiliated either with FUSE or his personal work.

Markmaking is a lifelong iterest of Neville Brody. For my first of several iterations, I chose to explore it in the convenience of digital space to find the somewhat primal effect he found so intriguing.

A face is often seen in his work, especially early in his career, the faces were obscured in some way. I took an image of Brody as a young-un and in photoshop I desaturated it and applied a threshold filter to achieve the two-tone black and white.

Once again experimenting with primality of human nature, I chose to include paint splatters to imply that the poster has been slung at carelessly. I obscured Brody's face by converting it to halftones and integrating it with the background - almost as if it were burnt - with a graphite-textured photoshop brush.

This inital experiment was the precursor for the concepts I used in the final.

Next Project:

Icon and Graphic Elements

Beginning with an object we had drawn for critique in Drawing I the previous semester, we designed an icon then picked a phrase associated with it to combine them in one black and white poster. The poster had to exhibit clear derivative from the Fibonacci sequence and effectiveness in black and white before optional completion in color.

Razor Sharp

This poster project was completed in tandem with the PBS Designer Series poster (which also had to be designed by the Fibonacci sequence), so it’s needless to say our poster designing skills were razor sharp by the end of the semester.

When developing the poster, we paid specific attention to the texture resulting from the text and icon, the Fibonacci sequence, and the visual narrative each of the elements created. Fairly soon into my creative process, I came up with the idea to use the words "razor sharp" layered over each other to look like bristly hair, and to use the razor to shave it. I divided the page down its diagonal to create two divisions that the razor’s handle would lie in the center of. The head of the razor is parallel to the opposite diagonal.

I treated the first third of the grid as the shaved part of the face - in the color version you can see a subtle skin texture overlayed. The combination of the saturated red and skin was intended to evoke a sense of danger or alert, implying that the razor is, in fact, razor sharp and will draw blood if used carelessly.

The two-part project began by visually exploring the object we drew as part of the Drawing I curriculum in order to create an icon which was functional on a large and small scale. It was certainly difficult at times to come up with creative, original, and dynamic iterations.

We were encouraged to produce a dynamic solution that would also be easily recognized as the object we'd gotten stuck with. Becuase of this, our professor had me change my object from an electric razor (which come in different forms and some have never come in contact with one). To nail the perspective - even after finding reference photos - I created a simple geometric 3D model to aid in my efforts.

Next, we selected our phrases, then studied our library of fonts to create a cohesive appearance which reflected the emotions evoked by the phrase.

Matière Texture Swatch

To study the capacity of material textures and the effect of their combination, we were assigned to create a single entity composed of two materials with unique tactile qualities. My swatch used candle wax and poplar planks.

Poplar Planks and Candle Wax

Melting the wax intrigued me, so I experimented with different factors’ effect on the cooling speed. I came to the conclusion that pouring wax onto water and a dry surface simultaneously would adhere the wax to the surface and quickly create a hardened structure that retained the likeness of the fluid state. Because wax repels water, the wax would unconditionally adhere to itself, lending the capability to create a complex wax labyrinth. I constructed a grid out of the planks which would serve as structural support for a 1x1 foot wax swatch. Then using clothespins, shoelace, and a ruler I created a mechanism to suspend the grid on the surface of the water (in a dishbin) so I could freely pour the wax onto both the water and the poplar
