The Jabberwocky

I created six illustrations bound together in a booklet based on the classic nonsense poem by Lewis Carroll.

Nonsense

’Twas brillig, and the slithy toves
Did gyre and gimble in the wabe:
All mimsy were the borogoves,
And the mome raths outgrabe.

“Beware the Jabberwock, my son!
The jaws that bite, the claws that catch!
Beware the Jubjub bird, and shun
The frumious Bandersnatch!”

He took his vorpal sword in hand;
Long time the manxome foe he sought—
So rested he by the Tumtum tree
And stood awhile in thought.

And, as in uffish thought he stood,
The Jabberwock, with eyes of flame,
Came whiffling through the tulgey wood,
And burbled as it came!

One, two! One, two! And through and through
The vorpal blade went snicker-snack!
He left it dead, and with its head
He went galumphing back.

“And hast thou slain the Jabberwock?
Come to my arms, my beamish boy!
O frabjous day! Callooh! Callay!”
He chortled in his joy.

’Twas brillig, and the slithy toves
Did gyre and gimble in the wabe:
All mimsy were the borogoves,
And the mome raths outgrabe.




Even before I began my analysis of the poem, I knew that I wanted to take a the poem on a walk down the opposite direction of Disney’s or Tim Burton’s take on the Alice in Wonderland canon. I wanted to make it decisively darker by drawing parallels between the actions of the hero and the sins of man. I adapted stylistic elements from the late horror artist Bernie Wrightson to create a more dubious and brooding atmosphere.

The poem is comprised of seven stanzas (I illustrated the latter six), which I drew parallel to each of the seven deadly sins in my planning process - in the order of Greed, Lust, Sloth, Envy, Wrath, Pride, and Gluttony respective to each stanza. These attributes shaped how I presented each illustration emotionally.

Analysis

The first Illustration deals with the hero’s greed manifest upon being sent to conquer and destroy.

The second represents his sloth at the base of the Tumtum tree. The scene presents his action of sloth and juxtaposes it with his persistence to act on greed. I placed emphasis on his hand to show it as his “vorpal sword,” the lack of a weapon is a metaphor for brutality of his own doing.

Acting on it’s envy, the Jabberwocky releases itself upon the hero where it is struck down in the name of the hero’s wrath.

The fourth illustration is from the Jabberwocky’s perspective to emphasize it as the victim of wrath as well as its own envy, and to evoke a sense of danger as the eyeless hero stares straight at the viewer.

Prideful as his conquest is completed, he arrives home to celebrate his success and fame.

In a deadly irony, his pride leads him to a dangerous intoxication.

I created a translation of the first and last stanza to clearly portray my interpretation:

It’s sunny, and the tree branches
Did sway in the wind:
All happy are our friends
And the wildlife reaches out

As the first stanza, it sets a scene of a forest with plentiful wildlife and coexisting establishment. As the last stanza, it shows nature running its course on the hero who, too intoxicated to defend himself, is graben by the force he grabed.

The booklet I created was intended to be a maze to flip through, making the viewing experience of the poem as confusing as the language used.

Photography

Photography is a passion of mine which bleeds greatly over into 3D Modeling. I find these two arts of composition fascinating because of their capacity to control a viewer’s bias because of it’s narrow portrayal of one subject.

Attention to Detail

Is the name of the game. Portraiture has the capability to present a person or animal in any emotional stance, where every variable from lighting, to angle, focal length and proximity can independently and subtly influence how they are percieved.

Abstract photography deals with a similar idea; in some instances, focusing an abnormal amount on a part of the subject may render the subject unrecognizable. In either case, details must be dealt with respect.

While Adobe Photoshop has the potential to change a photograph in amazing ways, there is a point on that spectrum where an image loses its integrity. These images have been minimally post-processed.

In the Style of Neville Brody

One of our final class projects involved an imaginary scenario: each of the students has been hired by PBS to design a poster for an episode in an upcoming series about the history of design. Each episode will cover a different designer who led the design of their time.

Research

Naturally, we drew designers out of a hat. I drew a particular rebel named Neville Brody.

Known for his early work for Fetish Records and his design for The Face Magazine in the 80’s and - later in the age of the internet - his leadership in digital font creation through FUSE and FontShop.

In his early days, Brody was a punk. He disdained his professors’ instruction, as they deemed his work “uncommercial.”

Inspiration

Selected works I chose to focus on, each are Brody's own.

Digital Ideation

Markmaking is a lifelong interest of Neville Brody. For my first of several iterations, I chose to explore the somewhat primal effect he found so intriguing.

A face is often seen in his work, especially early in his career, the faces were obscured in some way. I took an image of Brody as a young-un and in photoshop I desaturated it and applied a threshold filter to achieve the two-tone black and white.

Once again experimenting with primality of human nature, I chose to include paint splatters to imply that the poster has been slung at carelessly. I obscured Brody's face by converting it to halftones and integrating it with the background - almost as if it were burnt - with a graphite-textured photoshop brush.

This inital experiment was the precursor for the concepts I used in the final.

Poster Studies

Given a set body of text and a rigid corresponding hierarchy, I created four 11 x 17 graphic arrangements while adhering to the University of Cincinnati branding guidelines. Through this process, I gained the ability to justify my design decisions through rational and topical relevance while conveying the identity of an organization.

Inspiration

Sketches

Digital Ideation

For the first many iterations, we designed exclusively by a grid, and avoided particular graphic elements. Eventually, we were encouraged to spread out, to put personality and a wider variety of brand elements into our composition.

I pursued the idea of implied space and depth for my first graphic poster. For the appearance of a clean and finalized result, I used Autodesk Maya to emulate studio-lit photography with renderings that implied the depth of the paper. For a paper-like effect to project text on, I used 2- dimensional planes.

These were the least successful for two reasons: the official UC monograms become the visual focus - fighting the content and failing to show university affiliation, and the visual elements fail to reflect the content and purpose of the poster.

Final Poster

Striving for an overarching metaphor, I realized that my Bic ballpoint pen, laptop and mouse, scrap paper and lunatic scrawl could be universally recognized as a setup for design.

Next Project:

Body Mantle

The Poplar Prince’s robes were created from 101 twelve-inch poplar planks and held together with wood adhesive. The initial concept was modeled and rendered in Autodesk Maya, and the succeeding mantle was a loose adaption of what I could complete over the course of one all-nighter.

Research

Because the poplar planks resembled reeds, I researched styles of traditional reed armor - especially that of ancient eastern shoguns.

Sketches

Digital Ideation

I began by creating a duct tape bust of my torso that I could use to brainstorm, though, I opted to brainstorm in the digital space instead.

Construction

When I began to construct the mantle, I found that the skeleton made a better bust than my poplar-reinforced duct tape one. To create dimension of the overall form, a tiny (>.25in) spacer made of cut, layered, and glued poplar was placed between each plank. Without the spacers there would be no way to glue the sticks together. Each joint was held together for 30 minutes with a clothespin to avoid damage before the adhesive sealed.

The Poplar Prince

The model was meant to liken cascading and asymmetrical movement a nontraditional ebb from bottom right to upper left culminating in a fractal spiral on the left shoulder.
